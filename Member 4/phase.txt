Building a dedicated system with **Syft** is an excellent next step. While Trivy is a great "all-in-one" vulnerability scanner, Syft operates with a completely different architectural philosophy: it is a purpose-built, highly specialized cataloger designed to extract the deepest possible dependency data from your files, compiled binaries, and containers. 

Because Syft is solely focused on deep cataloging, **it does require distinct post-processing and companion tools** to achieve the same 21-attribute compliance you achieved with Trivy. Specifically, Syft operates entirely offline and does not scan for vulnerabilities on its own. 

Here is the exact phased roadmap to build your Syft-based system, incorporating the necessary post-processing steps:

### Phase 1: Tooling Architecture & Setup (The "START" Phase)
Unlike Trivy's single-binary execution, a Syft pipeline separates the generation, vulnerability scanning, and data enrichment into three distinct tools.
*   **Install Syft (The Cataloger):** This is your core engine. It will handle the layer-by-layer extraction of your target environments.
*   **Install Grype (The Vulnerability Scanner):** Syft requires its companion tool, Grype, to fulfil your client's Attribute 8 (Vulnerabilities). Grype is designed to natively consume Syft's output and correlate the components with known CVEs.
*   **Install `sbomify-action` (The Post-Processor):** Just like your Trivy system, Syft cannot natively find business data (like Release Dates or Suppliers). You must set up `sbomify-action` to handle this post-processing enrichment.

### Phase 2: Deep Extraction & Baseline Generation (The "PROGRESS" Phase)
This phase is where you execute Syft to see how its internal mechanics differ from Trivy.
*   **Step 1: Execute Syft.** Point Syft at your container image, raw filesystem, or compiled build artifacts. 
*   **How Syft Works Internally:** Instead of just looking for known package manifests, Syft executes a deep, **layer-by-layer analysis**. If you are scanning a container, it unpacks over thirty diverse package ecosystems (APK, DEB, RPM). Crucially, it parses complex edge cases that other scanners miss, such as extracting dependencies hidden inside deeply nested Java "uber-jars" or statically linked libraries compiled directly into Go and Rust binaries.
*   **Step 2: Format Output.** Instruct Syft to output this highly detailed inventory into a standard machine-readable format, such as **CycloneDX or SPDX**.

### Phase 3: Vulnerability Mapping (Post-Processing Step 1)
Because Syft only extracted the component names, versions, and hashes, you must now scan that list for security flaws.
*   **Step 3: Execute Grype.** Feed the CycloneDX/SPDX document generated by Syft directly into Grype. 
*   Grype will analyze the specific component versions identified by Syft, map them against global vulnerability databases, and inject the severity ratings and CVE identifiers into the SBOM to satisfy the vulnerability requirements.

### Phase 4: Business Data Enrichment (Post-Processing Step 2)
Your SBOM now has deep technical dependencies and vulnerability data, but it is still missing the business lifecycle data required by your client.
*   **Step 4: Execute `sbomify-action`.** Pass the Grype-updated SBOM through the `sbomify-action` toolkit.
*   The tool will automatically query authoritative external registries (such as PyPI, crates.io, and ClearlyDefined) to enrich the SBOM. It will inject the missing **Release Date, End-of-Life (EOL) Date, specific Supplier information, and precise License terms**.

### Phase 5: Attestation & Actionable Delivery (The "ADVANCE" Phase)
Now that you have a fully complete, 21-attribute SBOM generated via Syft, you must finalize the deliverables for the client.
*   **Step 5: Generate VEX / CSAF Documents.** Using the vulnerability data Grype injected, your security team must create a Vulnerability Exploitability eXchange (VEX) document. You will flag the vulnerabilities as *Not Affected, Affected, Fixed, or Under Investigation* so the client knows exactly what needs patching.
*   **Step 6: Cryptographic Signing.** Finally, cryptographically sign your completed Syft SBOM and VEX documents (using tools like Cosign or the *in-toto* framework) to prove to the client that the deep architectural blueprint has not been tampered with. 

**Summary of the Syft Workflow:**
**Target** $\rightarrow$ **Syft** *(Deep Component Extraction)* $\rightarrow$ **Grype** *(Vulnerability Injection)* $\rightarrow$ **sbomify-action** *(Lifecycle Data Enrichment)* $\rightarrow$ **Signed & Compliant SBOM.**
